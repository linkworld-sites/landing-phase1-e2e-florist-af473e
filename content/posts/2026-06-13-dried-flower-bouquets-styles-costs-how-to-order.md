A dried flower bouquet is not a compromise. It is a considered, lasting object — one that holds its shape and colour for months or years rather than days. Whether you are looking for a wedding bouquet that survives the photographs, a gift that will not wilt before it arrives, or a home arrangement that evolves beautifully with time, a well-made dried bouquet is one of the most quietly elegant things you can own.

## Popular Dried Flower Bouquet Styles

Dried flower bouquets are not a single aesthetic. The same raw materials — pampas grass, lunaria, strawflower, lavender — can be arranged into wildly different moods depending on structure, colour, and proportion. These are the styles we work with most often.

### Boho Dried Bouquet

The boho style is characterised by loose volume, textural contrast, and an intentionally undone quality. Pampas grass plumes sit alongside bunny tail grasses and trailing eucalyptus. Bleached honesty pods catch the light alongside dried cotton stems and feathery grasses. The palette tends toward naturals: ivory, sand, warm beige, and faded terracotta.

A boho dried bouquet works particularly well for outdoor weddings, festival-adjacent celebrations, and anyone who wants their bouquet to feel effortless rather than formal. It is the style most forgiving of asymmetry — in fact, slight irregularity is part of the point.

### Romantic Dried Bouquet

Romantic bouquets bring warmth and softness to the dried flower palette. Dried garden roses — their petals contracted slightly around the centre — pair with strawflowers in dusty pinks and peachy corals, dried lavender for depth, and silver brunia for a subtle shimmer. The structure is tighter than a boho bouquet but still organic: not stiff or architectural, but intentionally gathered.

This style suits spring and summer weddings, anniversaries, and gifts where you want to signal care and sentiment without formality. A romantic dried bouquet in blush, mauve, and sage reads as modern but warmly personal.

### Wild Dried Bouquet

The wild style takes its lead from what might actually grow together in a hedgerow or a dry summer meadow. Dried thistles, seed heads, grasses, and botanicals are arranged with minimal intervention — grouped loosely, stems visible and varied in length, nothing clipped or tidied. Dried wheat, eryngium, craspedia, and dried poppy heads all work well here.

A wild dried bouquet makes a strong visual statement. It is the most sculptural of the three styles and the most striking as a standalone arrangement. For weddings, it suits the confident couple who want something that photographs beautifully but does not look like it came from a standard florist.

## Which Flowers Work Best in a Dried Bouquet

Not every flower dries well. Some lose all their structure; others retain it perfectly and become even more interesting as they dry. Here are the botanicals that consistently perform:

**Pampas grass** is the defining texture of the contemporary dried bouquet. Its soft, feathery plumes — available in natural cream or dyed shades — provide immediate volume and a sense of movement. It is long-lasting, structurally stable, and widely available.

**Lunaria (honesty)** dries into translucent, papery pods that catch light in a way fresh flowers never can. The effect is ethereal rather than rustic. Lunaria adds a delicate, almost iridescent quality to arrangements and holds its form indefinitely.

**Strawflower** is one of the most reliable dried blooms available. The papery petals retain their colour beautifully — pinks, oranges, yellows, reds — and the flower holds its daisy-like form without any treatment. Strawflowers are the backbone of many romantic and boho arrangements.

**Lavender** brings both scent and structure. Dried lavender stems keep their fragrance for months, making a bouquet that is also a sensory object. The dusty purple-blue hue reads as calming and coherent, and lavender pairs well with almost everything.

**Bunny tail grass** adds lightness and movement. The small, soft seed heads cluster beautifully and provide contrast against larger, heavier botanicals like pampas or dried roses. They are also extremely long-lasting.

**Dried roses** retain their form and much of their colour when dried correctly, though they become more compact. The result is something denser and more jewel-like than a fresh rose — more sculptural, more indefinitely beautiful.

Dried flower bouquet costs vary depending on size, flower selection, complexity of arrangement, and whether it is custom-made or ready-made. These are representative UK price ranges:

DIY dried bouquets are substantially cheaper in raw materials (£15–£40 for a bridal-sized bouquet), but the cost in time and the learning curve involved in producing something that looks considered rather than homemade should factor into your decision.

## Care Tips for Your Dried Bouquet

A well-kept dried bouquet will last one to three years or longer. The key variables are light, moisture, and handling.

**Keep it out of direct sunlight.** UV exposure bleaches dried florals faster than anything else. Display your bouquet in a bright room but away from windows or spots that receive direct sun for part of the day.

**Avoid damp rooms.** Bathrooms, utility rooms, and rooms that experience condensation are poor environments for dried flowers. Moisture causes mould and softens the stems. A dry living room or bedroom is ideal.

**Dust carefully.** A light mist from a can of compressed air — held at arm's length — removes dust without disturbing the arrangement. Do not wipe dried flowers with a cloth, as the pressure will damage the delicate dried petals and seed heads.

**Do not add water.** This should go without saying, but dried bouquets need no water. Adding water will soften stems, encourage mould, and undo the drying process.

**Handle from the stems, not the flowers.** Dried florals are more fragile than fresh ones. When moving or repositioning your bouquet, always hold it by the bound stems rather than grasping the arrangement itself.
