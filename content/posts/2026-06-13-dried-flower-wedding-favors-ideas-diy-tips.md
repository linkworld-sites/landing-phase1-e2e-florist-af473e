Wedding favors have long been a source of stress: something chosen in a rush, wrapped in tulle, and abandoned on a venue chair by the end of the night. Dried flower wedding favors are different. They are something guests actually want to take home — a small, beautiful object that continues to look good weeks after the celebration.

Here is everything you need to know about dried flower wedding favors, from the most popular ideas to DIY instructions and honest cost estimates.

## Why Dried Flower Wedding Favors Are Trending

The shift toward dried florals in weddings has been building for several years, and it has extended naturally into the favor table.

**They are genuinely sustainable.** Fresh flower favors wilt within days and are often discarded. Dried florals last months or years, making them a more considered choice for couples who care about waste.

**They photograph beautifully.** Dusty pinks, faded terracottas, bleached honesty pods, pale lavender — dried flowers have a muted, organic palette that reads effortlessly on film. A favor table lined with mini dried bouquets is one of the strongest visual moments at a reception.

**They travel well.** Unlike fresh flowers, dried favors can be prepared weeks in advance, packaged flat, and transported without refrigeration. For destination weddings or couples managing logistics from a distance, this matters considerably.

**Guests keep them.** Dried flowers are decorative objects in their own right. A well-made mini bouquet or beautifully packaged sachet is something a guest will place on a shelf or windowsill — not leave behind at the venue.

## Top Dried Flower Wedding Favor Ideas

### Mini Dried Bouquets

A handful of stems bound with twine or satin ribbon is the most elegant dried flower favor available. Pampas grass, dried roses, eucalyptus, and lavender are all reliable choices. Keep each bouquet small — three to five stems — so it works as a standalone vase arrangement when guests arrive home.

A small tag looped around the binding with the couple's names and wedding date adds a personal touch without bulk.

### Seed Packets with Dried Blooms

Pair a hand-filled paper seed packet (lavender, wildflowers, or chamomile seeds work well) with a single pressed dried bloom tucked under a ribbon or placed inside a glassine window. Guests receive something alive to plant alongside something already beautiful. The combination of past and future suits a wedding particularly well.

### Pressed Flower Bookmarks

A laminated or resin-set pressed flower bookmark is a low-cost, high-impact favor that works especially well for book-loving couples. Press blooms from your wedding flowers (or matching varieties) between paper, allow two to three weeks to dry fully, then laminate or encase in thin resin strips. Punch a small hole, thread satin ribbon, and trim. The result is something genuinely lasting.

### Potpourri Sachets

A small organza or muslin sachet filled with dried lavender, rose petals, and a few drops of essential oil is a classic for good reason: it smells beautiful, lasts indefinitely, and costs very little to make in quantity. Tie with ribbon and attach a small sprig of dried lavender for a finished look.

## DIY Step-by-Step: Simple Dried Lavender Sachets

Making your own dried flower favors is manageable even without craft experience. These lavender sachets for up to 80 guests can be completed in a single afternoon.

**You will need:** dried lavender (100g covers approximately 20 sachets), small organza or muslin drawstring bags, a few drops of lavender essential oil, ribbon, and gift tags.

**Step 1 — Prepare the lavender.** If starting from fresh stems, hang bundles upside down in a warm, dry room for two to three weeks until fully dry. The stems should snap cleanly when bent. Pre-dried lavender from a supplier works equally well.

**Step 2 — Strip and scent the buds.** Run your fingers down each stem to collect the dried buds in a bowl. Stir two or three drops of lavender essential oil through the buds to refresh the scent. The oil absorbs quickly and will not stain fabric.

**Step 3 — Fill the sachets.** Use a small funnel or folded paper to pour approximately one tablespoon of buds into each bag. Do not overfill — the bag should close easily and sit flat.

**Step 4 — Tie and label.** Pull the drawstring closed, tie ribbon over the top, and attach a small gift tag. A sprig of dried lavender tucked under the ribbon completes the look in seconds.

**Step 5 — Display.** Arrange completed sachets in a shallow tray on the favor table, or place one on each place setting.

## Tips for Packaging and Presentation

**Keep it cohesive.** Choose one or two ribbon colours that match your wedding palette and use them consistently across every favor. Variation in ribbon colour makes a table look chaotic; consistency makes it look considered.

**Use height on the favor table.** Place taller mini bouquets at the back, sachets and bookmarks at the front. A stepped display — using small wooden crates or stacked books under a cloth — adds visual interest without effort.

**Label clearly but simply.** A small card reading "Please take one home" is enough. Guests should not have to wonder whether the display is decorative or intended for them.

**Wrap individual stems.** For mini bouquets, twist a small piece of tissue paper around the lower stems before binding with ribbon. It protects the delicate dried material during handling and adds a layer of refinement that guests notice.

**Prepare well in advance.** One of the greatest advantages of dried flower wedding favors is lead time. You can complete the entire favor table six to eight weeks before the wedding, store the finished pieces in dry conditions, and remove that task entirely from your final weeks of planning.
