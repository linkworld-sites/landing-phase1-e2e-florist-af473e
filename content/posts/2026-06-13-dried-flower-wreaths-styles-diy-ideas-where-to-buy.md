# Dried Flower Wreaths: Styles, DIY Ideas & Where to Buy

A dried flower wreath is one of the most enduring things you can hang on a door or wall. Where a fresh wreath demands weekly attention and wilts within days, a well-made dried botanical wreath holds its shape, colour, and texture for a year or more — often mellowing into something even more beautiful with age.

Over the last few years, dried flower wreaths have moved firmly into the interior design mainstream. You'll find them in Scandinavian-influenced hallways, maximalist dining rooms, and stripped-back cottages alike. The appeal is obvious: they're sculptural, zero-maintenance, and rich with the kind of organic texture that no artificial wreath can replicate.

This guide covers everything you need to know — popular styles and how to choose them, honest price ranges, a practical DIY walkthrough, how to keep your wreath looking its best, and where to source quality dried flower wreaths online.

## Popular Dried Flower Wreath Styles

Not all dried wreaths are the same. The botanical choices, base materials, and finishing details define the aesthetic entirely. Here are the styles worth knowing.

### Boho Dried Flower Wreaths

Boho wreaths lean into abundance and texture. Expect pampas grass plumes, bunny tail grass, lunaria (honesty) discs, bleached palm spears, and dried cotton pods arranged on a willow or grapevine base. The palette is warm and neutral — straw, cream, blush, and dusty terracotta — and the silhouette is usually full and slightly asymmetric, giving the piece a relaxed, effortless quality.

They work particularly well in:
- Boho or eclectic living rooms hung above a sofa or mantle
- Bedrooms over the headboard as a soft focal point
- Home offices where you want warmth without fuss

Look for wreaths with visible texture contrast: fine grasses against full plumes, smooth seed pods against fluffy cotton. That interplay is what makes a boho wreath feel designed rather than simply assembled.

### Rustic Dried Flower Wreaths

Rustic wreaths favour the familiar — wheat sheaves, dried lavender, eucalyptus, seed heads, and dried berries on a hessian-wrapped or bare twig base. The colours stay within the harvest palette: amber, sage, ochre, and deep burgundy.

These are the wreaths that look at home on a farmhouse front door, a kitchen dresser, or a dining room wall alongside woven baskets and wooden cutting boards. They're perennially popular in autumn but honestly work year-round if you avoid overtly seasonal accents (skip the mini pumpkins and they'll carry through spring).

**Rustic wreath hallmarks to look for:**
- Natural jute or hessian binding (not wire-visible)
- Earthy, muted tones without dyed botanicals
- Wheat, lavender, or dried herbs as the backbone

### Seasonal Dried Flower Wreaths

Seasonal wreaths pivot the botanical selection around the time of year without leaning into kitsch. A spring dried wreath might feature pale dried ranunculus, dried cherry blossom stems, and soft pink statice. A winter wreath might build on preserved eucalyptus, dried cotton, bleached seed pods, and silver brunia — festive without being gaudy.

The distinction from a "fresh" seasonal wreath is longevity: a seasonal dried wreath can stay up for the entire season (or beyond) without drooping. This makes them genuinely cost-efficient compared to fresh.

**Seasonal wreath suggestions by time of year:**
- **Spring:** Pale ranunculus, dried daisies, lavender, pale pink statice
- **Summer:** Bright strawflowers, dried sunflower heads, grasses, chamomile
- **Autumn:** Wheat, preserved oak leaves, dried berries, rosehips, orange craspedia
- **Winter:** Eucalyptus, cotton pods, dried oranges, brunia, bleached botanicals

### Minimalist and Sculptural Wreaths

A quieter category but a growing one — wreaths that prioritise form over abundance. A single variety, like dried lunaria arranged on a slim copper or wire ring, or a handful of dried pampas feathers on a bare twig circle, make a statement through restraint. These suit contemporary interiors where maximalism would feel out of place.

## Dried Flower Wreath Price Guide

Prices vary significantly depending on size, botanical content, and whether the wreath is handmade in small batches or mass-produced.

| Type | Approx. Price Range |
|------|-------------------|
| Small dried wreath (20–25 cm), simple style | £18–£35 |
| Medium dried wreath (30–35 cm), mixed botanicals | £35–£65 |
| Large statement wreath (40–50 cm) | £65–£120 |
| Bespoke / handmade artisan wreath | £80–£200+ |

A few things to bear in mind when assessing price:

**Base materials matter.** A willow or grapevine base is more durable and holds botanicals better than foam or polystyrene rings. If a listing doesn't specify the base, ask.

**Dyed vs. naturally dried.** Intensely bright or jet-black botanicals are almost always dyed. That's not inherently bad, but dyed pieces tend to fade faster and less gracefully than naturally dried ones. Naturally dried botanicals age to a warmer, richer version of themselves.

**Handmade vs. mass-produced.** Mass-produced dried wreaths often use lower-grade botanicals that shed more heavily and lose texture faster. Handmade wreaths from specialist florists or botanical studios use better-quality stems and more considered composition.

## How to Make a Dried Flower Wreath: DIY Step-by-Step

Making your own dried flower wreath is more achievable than it looks, and far cheaper than buying a comparable piece at retail. A 30 cm finished wreath typically costs £12–£20 in materials.

### What You'll Need

- **Wreath base:** Willow, grapevine, or a wire ring (available from craft suppliers; 25–35 cm is a practical size for a first attempt)
- **Dried botanicals:** Choose 4–6 varieties for visual interest. A good starter mix: pampas grass, bunny tail grass, dried lavender, dried statice, and a focal bloom like dried craspedia or dried rosebuds
- **Floral wire:** 0.45 mm gauge wire for securing stems
- **Floral tape:** Green, for binding
- **Wire cutters or strong scissors**
- **Ribbon or twine:** For hanging
- **Hot glue gun:** Optional but useful for securing lightweight botanicals

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**1. Prepare your botanicals.** If you're drying your own flowers, hang them upside down in small bunches in a warm, dark, well-ventilated space for 2–4 weeks before use. If using pre-dried stems, remove any damaged heads and cut stems to a consistent working length of 12–15 cm.

**2. Sort into bunches.** Group stems by variety into small bunches of 3–5 stems each. Having your bunches pre-assembled speeds up the process significantly.

**3. Establish the first layer.** Starting at the top of your wreath base, wire a bunch of your background filler (lavender, grasses, or statice work well) flat against the frame, with stems pointing clockwise. Secure with two or three winds of floral wire.

**4. Overlap and build.** Place the next bunch so it overlaps the wire binding of the first, hiding it completely. Continue around the frame, maintaining even spacing and consistent stem direction. Work in one direction all the way around.

**5. Add focal botanicals last.** Once the base layer covers the frame, place your statement flowers (dried rosebuds, craspedia, dried ranunculus) on top. Secure with wire or a small dot of hot glue for delicate heads.

**6. Fill gaps.** Step back and identify any sparse areas. Fill with small bunches of fine grass or seed heads to give an even, full appearance.

**7. Attach hanging hardware.** Tie a length of ribbon or a loop of twine through the top of the frame. Raffia or linen twine keeps the look natural; velvet ribbon adds a touch of luxury.

**Beginner tips:**
- Work on a flat surface rather than holding the wreath — it's much easier to judge balance
- Don't over-tighten wire; dried stems are brittle and will snap under pressure
- A fuller wreath generally looks more polished than a sparse one
- Step back regularly — close-up, imperfections look worse than they are

## Caring for Your Dried Flower Wreath

Dried wreaths are low-maintenance but not zero-maintenance. A few simple habits will extend a quality wreath's life considerably.

### Placement

**Avoid direct sunlight.** UV exposure is the single biggest enemy of dried botanicals — it bleaches colours and makes stems brittle. A wreath hung on a south-facing door in full sun will fade noticeably within weeks. A hallway or sheltered porch position will preserve it far longer.

**Avoid high humidity.** Bathrooms and kitchens aren't ideal. Moisture can cause dried botanicals to rehydrate slightly and then re-dry at odd angles, losing their original form. If you want a wreath in a kitchen, hang it away from the steam zone above the hob.

**Front doors are fine** — but seasonal exposure to rain and wind will accelerate ageing. Many dried wreath owners rotate their front door wreath indoors when heavy weather is forecast.

### Cleaning

Dust with a hairdryer on the lowest cool setting, held at arm's length, or use a very soft artist's brush on individual blooms. Never wet-clean a dried wreath — water rehydrates the stems and can cause mould in the interior of the base.

### How Long Do Dried Flower Wreaths Last?

In a sheltered, low-humidity, low-UV position: **1–3 years** before significant fading or shedding occurs. In a sunny doorway or humid room, expect 6–12 months before you'll want to refresh the piece.

Naturally dried botanicals (lavender, wheat, grasses) tend to outlast dyed or artificially preserved pieces. Pampas grass and bunny tails are particularly durable; dried ranunculus and roses are more delicate.

Some shedding is entirely normal, especially in the first few weeks as the wreath settles. It doesn't indicate poor quality — it's the nature of dried botanicals.

## Where to Buy Dried Flower Wreaths Online

The market for dried flower wreaths online has grown enormously, which is good news for buyers — but it also means quality varies widely. Here's how to navigate it.

### What to Look for in an Online Supplier

**Clear photography, multiple angles.** A reputable seller shows the wreath from the front, the side, and ideally in situ — so you can judge depth, fullness, and real-world scale.

**Stated dimensions.** Always check the diameter. A 20 cm wreath looks very different on a large front door than a 40 cm one, and listings can be misleading if you're not checking the measurements.

**Botanical transparency.** The listing should name the flowers and foliages used, and ideally indicate whether they're naturally dried or dyed.

**Provenance and production.** Look for shops that describe their process — where the botanicals are sourced, whether wreaths are handmade to order or batch-produced. This usually signals the supplier cares about quality.

**Returns policy.** Dried wreaths are fragile and can be damaged in transit. A supplier confident in their packaging will offer a fair returns or replacement policy.

### Types of Sellers Worth Exploring

- **Independent botanical florists:** Often the best quality. Look for florists specialising in dried work — they'll have better botanical selection and more considered design.
- **Craft marketplaces (Etsy, Not on the High Street):** Wide range of quality; read reviews carefully and look at shop history.
- **Botanical studio brands:** A growing category of studio-led brands that produce dried and preserved botanicals as a core offer rather than a sideline to fresh flowers.
