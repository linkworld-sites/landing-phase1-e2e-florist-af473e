Your wedding bouquet holds something irreplaceable: the exact flowers you chose, the colours that defined your day, the scent of the morning you became married. Preserving it is not about nostalgia — it is about giving beauty a longer life.

This guide walks you through the most effective methods for preserving a wedding bouquet, what to expect from each, and when to trust the work to a professional studio.

## Why Preserve Your Wedding Bouquet?

Fresh flowers last four to seven days. A well-preserved bouquet can last decades — or longer. Beyond sentiment, preserved florals become a design element in your home: framed behind glass, arranged under a dome, or set into a resin block on a windowsill that catches the afternoon light.

Preservation also lets you hold on to the specific varieties that were in season on your wedding day — peonies, garden roses, eucalyptus — long after the season turns.

## Top Methods for Preserving Your Wedding Bouquet

### 1. Air Drying — The Classic Method

Air drying is the simplest and most accessible approach. It works best for hardier flowers with robust petals.

**How it works:** Remove the bouquet tie, strip lower leaves, and hang it upside down in a warm, dry, well-ventilated space away from direct sunlight. Drying takes two to four weeks depending on the density of the blooms.

**Best for:** Roses, lavender, globe amaranth, strawflower.

**Limitations:** Colours fade significantly, and delicate petals — peonies, sweet peas, ranunculus — tend to shrivel or drop. The result is beautiful in a rustic way, but not colour-true.

### 2. Silica Gel — For Better Colour Retention

Silica gel is a desiccant that draws moisture from petals while preserving their shape. Crucially, it retains far more of the flower's original colour than air drying.

**How it works:** Pour a 1–2 cm layer of silica gel crystals into an airtight container. Lay flowers face-up and gently pour more crystals around and over each bloom until fully covered. Seal and leave for three to seven days. Remove carefully with a soft brush.

**Best for:** Roses, dahlias, zinnias, carnations.

**Limitations:** Petals remain fragile after drying and require careful handling. Very large or dense blooms (full hydrangea heads, for example) are difficult to treat evenly at home.

### 3. Resin Preservation — A Modern Keepsake

Resin encapsulation has become one of the most sought-after ways to preserve wedding flowers. Individual petals or whole blooms are set into clear epoxy resin, creating objects — paperweights, coasters, jewellery, framed panels — that will outlast generations.

**How it works:** Flowers must be fully dried first (via silica gel or air drying) before going into resin. Wet flowers trapped in resin will brown and decompose over time. The drying, casting, and curing process takes two to four weeks in professional hands.

**Best for:** Any fully dried bloom; particularly striking with roses, dried hydrangea, and pressed ferns.

**Limitations:** Difficult to achieve professional results at home without practice. Bubbles, clouding, and yellowing are common when casting conditions are not carefully controlled.

## Timeline and Tips for Each Method

| Method | Preparation | Drying or Curing | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air Drying | 15 minutes | 2–4 weeks | Rustic, muted |
| Silica Gel | 30 minutes | 3–7 days | Vivid colour |
| Resin | Pre-dry first | 2–4 weeks total | Sculptural keepsake |

**Tips that apply to every method:**

- Start preservation within 24 to 48 hours of your wedding. Wilted flowers do not preserve well regardless of method.
- Remove any damaged or browning petals before you begin — they will only look worse with time.
- Store work-in-progress at consistent temperature. Humidity swings are the enemy of good preservation.
- Silica gel crystals are reusable: heat them in an oven at 120°C for one hour to reactivate and draw out absorbed moisture.

## When to Start — Timing Is Everything

The single most important factor in bouquet preservation is how quickly you begin. Flowers start their decline the moment they are cut, and that process accelerates after a long day in a warm venue.

If you are planning to preserve your bouquet yourself, ask a trusted person to begin the process on your wedding evening or the morning after. If you are handing your bouquet to a professional studio, most accept flowers within 72 hours of the ceremony. Some studios offer a postal service — pack your bouquet in a box with tissue paper (never plastic wrap, which traps moisture) and ship it overnight.
