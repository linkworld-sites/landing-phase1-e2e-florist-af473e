Dried Flower Centerpieces: Ideas, Costs & How to DIY

By Petal & Keep
June 11, 2026
6 min read

Dried Flower Centerpiece Guide

A centerpiece is a promise made to your guests before they sit down. It sets the tone for the table, the meal, the whole occasion. And when that centerpiece is built from dried flowers, it offers something fresh arrangements simply cannot: it looks just as beautiful the morning after as it did the evening before — and, handled well, for months to come.

Dried flower centerpieces have moved well beyond the dusty country-kitchen aesthetic of two decades ago. Today, they are a genuine alternative to fresh florals across weddings, dinner parties, and home tablescaping — with a growing range of varieties, a compelling cost story, and a straightforwardness that makes DIY genuinely viable. Here is everything you need to know.

The Best Dried Flowers for Centerpieces

Not every dried variety has what it takes to anchor a table. The best performers share a combination of visual presence, structural integrity, and the ability to hold their form over days or weeks without a water source.

Pampas Grass

Pampas has become the signature of modern dried flower styling — and for good reason. The feathered plumes are dramatically tactile, they photograph with movement even when completely still, and a single stem can carry an entire centerpiece. For tall arrangements, pampas creates the kind of airy column that makes a dining table feel genuinely special. For lower centerpieces, clip a plume short and let it spill across the arrangement. Natural pampas ranges from ivory to warm honey; bleached varieties offer a cooler, sculptural look. A note on shedding: keep pampas away from air vents and heavy foot traffic during an event — the plumes will stay intact, but they are not robust to physical contact.

Strawflowers (Helichrysum)

If pampas provides the drama, strawflowers provide the colour and detail. Helichrysum dries exceptionally well, retaining vivid pigment for months — burnt orange, deep burgundy, chalky pink, bright yellow — which makes them invaluable when you need a centerpiece that reads boldly across a room. Their papery petals have a satisfying crispness that looks intentional rather than withered. Use them massed together in low bowls for an Ikebana-adjacent effect, or scattered through taller arrangements to add focal points amid softer textures.

Lunaria (Honesty)

Lunaria is the secret weapon. The dried seed pods — translucent, silver-white, roughly the diameter of a 50p coin — catch candlelight and low ambient light in a way that nothing else in the dried flower world quite replicates. They shimmer. At an evening event, a centerpiece that includes lunaria becomes visibly different once the room dims: the pods glow softly, picking up the warmth of the flame. Lunaria is delicate compared to pampas or strawflowers, so handle with care, but its visual payoff is outsized relative to its cost.

Dried vs. Fresh: What Do Centerpieces Actually Cost?

Cost is one of the most significant practical arguments for dried flower centerpieces. Fresh flower arrangements require a florist to work the morning of the event, stems sourced within days of use, refrigerated transport, and either collection or disposal afterwards. That infrastructure is reflected in the price.

Item Fresh Flowers Dried Flowers
Mid-range table centerpiece (per table) €65–€120 €30–€65
Florist labour (event of 10 tables) €300–€600 DIY viable — €0 labour
Last-minute replacements / wastage Common — up to 15% Near-zero
Post-event use Discard within 48 hrs Display for months

"We ordered our dried centerpieces three weeks before the wedding. They arrived, we arranged them the afternoon before, and they looked exactly the same two days after the event. It was the easiest part of the whole day."

For events with more than eight tables, the cost difference between fresh and dried can be substantial. The ability to prepare dried centerpieces days — or even weeks — in advance also removes the last-minute logistics that make event floristry stressful. 3 DIY Styling Tips for Dried Flower Centerpieces

Dried flowers reward a slightly different approach to arranging than fresh florals. The following three principles will take a centerpiece from passable to genuinely beautiful — no floristry training required.

Tip 01

Build the skeleton first, fill second

Before you place a single bloom, establish the structure of your arrangement using your tallest, most architectural stems — pampas grass, dried allium, cotton stems. These create the silhouette and determine the height. Once the skeleton is in place, add your mid-layer florals (dried roses, strawflowers) to build density, and finish with the most delicate elements (lunaria, bunny tails) as a final accent layer. This sequence prevents you from burying your most beautiful material at the back.

Tip 02

Use odd numbers and asymmetry deliberately

Symmetrical centerpieces can feel static. For a more organic, gathered-from-the-field quality, work in odd groupings — three pampas plumes rather than two or four — and allow the arrangement to have a slight lean or tilt. A centerpiece that looks as though it grew naturally from its vessel reads as effortless. Resist the impulse to even everything out: the slight imbalance is where the character lives.

Tip 03

Match your vessel to the texture of your stems

The vessel is as much a part of the arrangement as the flowers. Raw terracotta and matte ceramic work beautifully with the earth tones of pampas and dried roses. Clear glass vessels allow lunaria and bunny tails to show their delicate stems. Tall, narrow-mouthed vessels naturally support loose, spilling arrangements; wide-mouthed bowls suit dense, low compositions. If in doubt, a simple terracotta pot in a neutral tone will complement almost every dried variety without competing for attention.
